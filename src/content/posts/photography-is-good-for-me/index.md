On Sunday, I bought a film camera at the flea market.
From some cursory research, it wasn't the best price for the particular model, and when I asked the seller what his return policy was,
the answer was basically "trust me, you won't need to return it."
And I wasn't even sure a film camera was what I wanted (I thought I would start with a digital camera).

My thought was though, I could wait and find a better deal on Facebook Marketplace, but who knows when I'll actually get around to doing that.
So better to buy one at the market since I was already there.

And so I bought it, and I spent the rest of the day taking pictures with it.

(image: canon ae-1 program)

On the same day that I got the camera, I used up all 36 exposures of my first roll of film
(going to the beach helped), and the next day I took the film to the lab.
The anticipation and the joy of receiving the scans was like Christmas.

(images)

I'd thought that not being able to see my results immediately would be a detriment,
but it ended up being something I found beautiful.
The added process, the necessity of going to the photo lab and waiting for the scans to be ready,
made it feel so much more rewarding.
It was a similar feeling to completing a piece of art after spending much time working on it
or finally understanding a difficult problem after spending time thinking about it.
The delay in payoff is what makes these things so satisfying.

As I write this, I realize it had been a while since I've done something in my spare time like this.
These days, I've been keeping busy by going out to bars and trying new restaurants.
(And I waste a lot of time scrolling.)
I'm realizing now that it had been a while since I had invested significant time and energy into my hobbies.

Somehow, I have a hard time sticking to my hobbies.
I used to be consistent with going to the gym, but not anymore;
I never seem to feel motivated to open any of the books sitting on my shelf;
I haven't baked something in ages.
And so I haven't felt that feeling of satisfaction that comes with putting effort into something for a while.

So getting into photography is a welcome departure from this period of dullness.
And I think that it makes me happy in other ways too. 

Somehow, having a camera in my hands makes me a more social person.
Now, I was by no means an introverted person before this.
But holding a camera gives me a reason to be in a space and an excuse to talk to people.
At the beach, I saw a young man fishing.
Taking pictures of the ocean gave me an excuse to stand next to him as he fished, and we struck up a conversation.
It's sort of like how holding a drink in your hand at a party makes it easier to approach people.

And photography got me to get my steps in.
Today, taking photos in Brooklyn and Central Park, I totaled 28,104 steps, much higher than my average (which I will not disclose).
And I discovered the Conservatory Garden, which was absolutely beautiful!

I bought a bunch of film today, so I hope to continue to get out there, find cool places, talk to people, and take cool pictures.
Stay tuned for more photography blog posts in the future!
